            <h4>The biggest dog, tallest dog, shortest dog &amp; smallest dog 
              in the world</h4></td>
          <td align="right">

          <td width="177" rowspan="5" class="articletext"><p class="smallprint"> Apr 15, 2007</p>
            <p>What's the difference between the largest dog in the world and the smallest dog in the world? Aside from the obvious (284 lbs, give or take 27 oz), the answer is: not a lot.</p>
            <p>Despite the fact that &quot;Hercules&quot; (284 lbs) is 200 times more dog than &quot;Danka&quot; (27 oz), the difference is merely a single genetic variant of the <em>IGF1</em> gene, a variant which small dogs possess and large dogs lack.</p>
            <p>An international team of 21 geneticists working with the <a href="http://www.genome.gov/" target="_blank">National Human Genome Research Institute</a>, published its findings last Friday in the journal <a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/short/316/5821/112" target="_blank"><em>Science</em></a> after having studied DNA samples of over 3,000 dogs and 143 breeds.</p>
            <p>Many of these samples were taken by swabbing the subject's cheek with one hand while waving a tennis ball with the other as a distraction (clever lot, those geneticists).</p>
            <p>Dogs proved to be the ideal subjects due to their variety of sizes, &quot;the greatest diversity in body size of any mammalian species.&quot; <sup>1</sup> And of course, dogs respond much better to tennis balls than, say, pea plants do.</p>
            <p>The researchers' findings have been hailed as a great advancement in our understanding of certain genetic proclivities such as prostate cancer, also linked to the <em>IGF1</em> gene. </p>
            <p>Future aims of the study are to expand into other aspects of   canine morphology such as leg length and skull shape.</p>
            <p>Who knows, maybe one day we may actually figure out why dogs have thumbs. <img src="../img/paw.gif" width="14" height="16" align="texttop" /></p></td>
