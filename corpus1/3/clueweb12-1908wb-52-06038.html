  • You Can't Rescue the Financial System If You Can't Read a Balance Sheet

    Seeded on Wed Oct 1, 2008 1:45 PM EDT (hussmanfunds.com)

    The only way that buying the questionable assets will increase capital on the liability side of the balance sheet is if the Treasury overpays for them.

    A better approach would be for the government to provide capital directly, in the form of a "super-bond," in an amount no greater than the debt to bondholders. The "super-bond" would be subordinate to customer liabilities, so it could be counted as capital for the purpose of capital requirements, and would be seen by customers as a legitimate cushion of protection. However, in the event of bankruptcy, it would have a senior claim in front of both stockholders and even senior bondholders. Do that, and you've actually got a mechanism to protect the financial system while at the same time protecting customers and taxpayers. Ideally, the super-bond accrues a relatively high rate of interest so that financials have an incentive to shift to private financing as soon as possible, but you would also defer the interest until the bank meets a minimal level of profitability to make sure that the financing doesn't strain the institution's liquidity.

The Global Compact has brought governments, aid agencies, and giant multinationals together to coordinate approaches to the eradication of want.

Smaller countries of the West will be especially hit. Countries like Italy which today are economic giants could especially be at threat.

    Take the issue of resources. China's growth has already driven resource markets wild. Prices have gone higher for
    these inputs and are expected to grow higher. The size of domestic sourcing pools for resources will count more. Let's take talent for example. People like Bill Gates already reckon that the United States does not have enough of a supply of certain technical personnel to keep its competitive advantage in technology. If this is true, what does this say about Belgium, or Finland or Sweden? It seems that for every single resource, the ability to find cheap sources at home is going to count more.

    Did you notice that on the list of the Next-11, the countries that would come into the club of the world's largest, most powerful economies, one factor is the single common denominator. Population and land mass. Most of them have huge, growing populations and expanses of land mass. Most of these countries have huge resources and are creating new sources of them all the time. How much land exists in Switzerland for growing corn for biofuels.

    Is the growth of the newly emerging economies a threat to the old ones? If you believe that the rich world keeps the poor world poor, you're essentially saying this. Or at least you are saying that the rich world sees it as such.

    This is not Tom Friedman's flat world. If anything this view of the world is of a pyramid. A pyramid with a very wide base and narrow peak. Yet there is enough in the world today that shows that Tom Friedman's view, much as it has come under heavy fire recently, does have some value to it.

    Let's take the theoretical view first. In theory, a globalised world favour the bottom of the pyramid almost as much as it benefits those at the rarefied heights of its peak. Much as Western corporations expand their value chains worldwide and develop complex but extremely efficient flows of value across territories and jurisdiction to bridge opportunities and breach limits, the flattening of markets allows poor small-scale but organised producers in developing countries to sell in developed markets.

    These opportunities also exist in practice. The challenge is in orientating developing countries to fulfil them. India's offshoring industry might be dominated by large corporations but cooperatives of poor but educated people in Kenya, if backed by technology, can compete for lower-end service processing. Envelope stuffing for example.

    In this view, there is no reason for growth in one part to take food off tables in another part. The evidence available about this is quite substantial too. Our world, especially our world of consumption has become very much interconnected and interdependent.

    On the other hand what can be said about countries that consume more than their size. Yes this refers to the United States. Take any commodity whatsoever, and America consumes more per capita than any other country. Of course the rise of China has changed this. China now consumes more of so many things that does the United States. Now that we have mentioned both China and the United States, you know what is coming next. The point that will follow is that the rise of newer economic powerhouses, even if they do not consume at the American rate would in some way threaten the price of US consumption.

    We all use the same inputs sourced from a limited font. Production of copper, tungsten, oil, palm oil, starch, sillicon, etc is limited. Increase in demand brings up some few issues.

    1. Standard of living. In the rich West, standard of living is directly related to the number of goods and services available for consumption. One of the most solemn vows of every Western government is to protect the standard of living of its people as defined. This standard of living can however be threatened by market forced reductions in consumption. If the price of petrol increases enough, Americans would eventually drive less, and so consume fewer fast foods, which would consume less something or the other.

    Do not get anything written here wrong. The tone is not fatalist. I am with the optimists. I have been very deeply impressed with Bjorn Lomborg and his New Optimism. I hate his politics related to the Kyoto Protocol. Nevertheless, he has been quite right in pointing out that mobilising resources and keeping the flow of trade and economic growth going is the approach. He has also been quite right that in the end, the world has in reality been much better off than 50 years ago. He points out that all the pessimistic predictions of the future of the last 40 years have been very wrong. He shows how even optimists' best hopes have been surpassed.

    Still it just seems that there would have to be a period of adjusting for some countries. In France for example, people are saying that is appears that, for the first time in history, we would have a generation live worse than their parents did. In the United States where growth in the Bush years has been driven by cheap consumer loans and consumption, the non-arrival of another 1990s productivity bump might see standards of living decline.

    2. The Military Dimension. The Suez Crisis showed how much economic interests are tied to military interests.   • Milton Friedman: a study in failure

    Seeded on Sun Nov 19, 2006 6:45 PM EST (Guardian Unlimited)

    A new interest of mine is in following what people are saying about and how they are judging Milton Friedman's legacy as an Economist. It's one of those silly, and illogical quarks of us Economists.

    Milton Friedman, who has died aged 94, was not the most important economist of the post-war era - that title belongs to the brilliant Paul Samuelson - but he was certainly the most controversial. Yet despite his views being championed by so many politicians on the right, it may come as a surprise that Friedman's career as a policymaker largely ended in failure.

    Given his status as a long-standing hate figure, the assumption by many of the left is that his agenda was cemented into place during the Reagan and Thatcher administrations in the early 1980s, especially Friedman's well-known view that inflation is solely influenced by changes in the money supply. But very few of Friedman's most cherished proposals were ever put in to practice. Of those that where - such as monetarism - almost all turned into failure.

    The great irony for Friedman's fans is that the one piece of public policy he was responsible for that was widely and internationally adopted was one that greatly increased the ability of central governments to collect taxes - a policy he later repudiated in disgust.

    Obituaries of Friedman will doubtlessly sing of his successes. But close examination will show them to be few, and none unalloyed. For all his high public profile - thanks to his regular column in Newsweek and series on US television, Free To Choose, which made him into something of a star - today no mainstream academic economist is a monetarist and Friedman left no lasting school of academic heirs. Even the "Chicago school" at the University of Chicago has waned in influence, eclipsed by the mighty MIT army of economists that followed Samuelson.

    Of course Friedman is greatly respected for his theoretical work as an economist, especially on his analysis of the role of money, the importance of inflation expectations in wages and employment, and perhaps his most lasting contribution (it could be argued), the permanent income hypothesis, which suggests that households take a longer view of anticipating their past and future income than previously thought.
