     <BR>
     A capital gain is income derived from the sale of an investment. A capital
    investment can be a home, a farm, a ranch, a family business, or a work of
    art, for instance. In most years slightly less than half of taxable capital
    gains are realized on the sale of corporate stock. The capital gain is the
    difference between the money received from selling the asset and the price
    paid for it. <BR>
     <BR>
     &quot;Capital gains&quot; tax is really a misnomer. It would be more
    appropriate to call it the &quot;capital formation&quot; tax. It is a tax
    penalty imposed on productivity, investment, and capital accumulation. <BR>
     <BR>
     The capital gains tax is different from almost all other forms of taxation
    in that it is a voluntary tax. Since the tax is paid only when an asset is
    sold, taxpayers can legally avoid payment by holding on to their assets--a
    phenomenon known as the &quot;lock-in effect.&quot; <BR>
     <BR>
     There are many unfairnesses imbedded in the current tax treatment of
    capital gains. One is that capital gains are not indexed for inflation: the
    seller pays tax not only on the real gain in purchasing power but also on
    the illusory gain attributable to inflation. The inflation penalty is one
    reason that, historically, capital gains have been taxed at lower rates than
    ordinary income. In fact, &quot;most capital gains were not gains of real
    purchasing power at all, but simply represented the maintenance of principal
    in an inflationary world.&quot; <BR>
     <BR>
     Another unfairness of the tax is that individuals are permitted to deduct
    only a portion of the capital losses that they incur, whereas they must pay
    taxes on all of the gains. That introduces an unfriendly bias in the tax
    code against risk taking. When taxpayers undertake risky investments, the
    government taxes fully any gain that they realize if the investment has a
    positive return. But the government allows only partial tax deduction if the
    venture goes sour and results in a loss. <BR>
     <BR>
     There is one other large inequity of the capital gains tax. It represents a
    form of double taxation on capital formation. This is how economists Victor
    Canto and Harvey Hirschorn explain the situation: <BR>
     <BR>
     A government can choose to tax either the value of an asset or its yield,
    but it should not tax both. Capital gains are literally the appreciation in
    the value of an existing asset. Any appreciation reflects merely an increase
    in the after-tax rateof return on the asset. The taxes implicit in the
    asset's after-tax earnings are already fully reflected in the asset's price
    or change in price. Any additional tax is strictly double taxation. <BR>
     <BR>
     Take, for example, the capital gains tax paid on a pharmaceutical stock.
    The value of that stock is based on the discounted present value of all of
    the future proceeds of the company. If the company is expected to earn
    Rs.100,000 a year for the next 20 years, the sales price of the stock will
    reflect those returns. The &quot;gain&quot; that the seller realizes from
    the sale of the stock will reflect those future returns and thus the seller
    will pay capital gains tax on the future stream of income. But the company's
    future Rs.100,000 annual returns will also be taxed when they are earned. So
    the Rs.100,000 in profits is taxed twice--when the owners sell their shares
    of stock and when the company actually earns the income. That is why many
    tax analysts argue that the most equitable rate of tax on capital gains is 
    zero. </DIV></DIV>
